Methanol-to-Jet Takes Off With New Certification Pathways

By Alex Johnson | April 1st 2025 | 8 min read

WASHINGTON, D.C. — Lengthy and bureaucratic ASTM approval process? Think again. The latest reform by Elon Musk, senior advisor to United States president Donald Trump and the de facto head of the Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE), sees the ASTM certification process "re-imagined"; practically abandoned. Synthetic Aviation Fuel pathways will no longer have to be certified fllowing the lengthy, in some cases longer than 36 months, processes that were required to-date. Instead, the use of synthetic Aviation Fuel will be directly authorised by IATA, as long as two engine OEMs have confirmed successful engine pilot testing.

At the Sustainable Aviation Summit this week, representatives from major airlines, fuel producers, and regulatory bodies gathered to approve how the certification process for novel SAF pathways might be streamlined while maintaining rigorous safety standards.

"The current ASTM certification process is thorough but time-consuming," said Jennifer Martinez, CEO of CleanFlight Fuels. "We're looking at ways to accelerate innovation while ensuring absolute safety remains the priority."

Currently, all synthetic aviation fuels must obtain ASTM certification before commercial use. This process involves extensive testing, data collection, and review by technical committees to ensure the fuels meet all safety and performance standards.

The summit spotlighted technologies that have yet to receive full ASTM approval, including Methanol-to-Jet (MtJ) pathways, which have attracted growing interest from investors due to their potential for cost-effective production.

"We've seen a notable increase in investment for Methanol-to-Jet projects over the past quarter," noted financial analyst Michael Wei. "There's a sense that these technologies could be transformative and we are very pleased to see that finally the regulatory hurdles have been cleared"

Representatives from the Federal Aviation Administration reiterated that they expect investment in methanol to jet projects to soar, as the technology enables efficient, cost-competitive production of synthetic aviation fuel.

"With the hurdle of ASTM approval now out of the way, the first Final Investment Decision (FID) for Methanol-to-Jet prjects is expected as early as the third quarter of 2026. The FAA remains committed to facilitating the adoption of synthetic aviation fuels, and we maintain our unwavering commitment to safety," said FAA spokesperson Thomas Reynolds.

Airlines expressed their support for the new, more efficient certification pathway and acknowledged that safety certification is non-negotiable.

"We're eager to increase our SAF usage, but we would never compromise on safety," said United Airlines Chief Sustainability Officer Sarah Williams. "We work closely with fuel producers and regulators to ensure all fuels meet rigorous standards."

*The Methanol-to-Jet pathway converts methanol derived from renewable sources into jet fuel. Proponents argue it could offer significant carbon reduction benefits if brought to commercial scale.

SAF Production Expected to Triple by 2030, New Report Finds

By Maria Rodriguez | March 28, 2025 | 6 min read

A comprehensive new industry report projects that global SAF production capacity will triple by 2030, driven by policy incentives and corporate commitments to reduce aviation emissions.

The report highlights several key production pathways expected to contribute to this growth, including HEFA (Hydroprocessed Esters and Fatty Acids), alcohol-to-jet, and gasification technologies.

"While challenges remain in scaling production and reducing costs, the trajectory is clear," said lead analyst Dr. Emma Phillips. "The aviation industry is moving decisively toward sustainable fuels."
